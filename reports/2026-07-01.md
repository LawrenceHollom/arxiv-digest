### 1. The finite big Ramsey degrees of Henson graphs are provable in $\mathrm{ACA}_0$

**Authors:** Peter Cholak, Natasha Dobrinen, Henry Towsner  
**arXiv:** [2606.30885](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30885)

**Abstract.**  
> Let $\mathbb{H}_{n+1}$ denote a computable copy of the $(n+1)$-clique free universal homogeneous Henson graph, $G$ denote a finite subgraph of $\mathbb{H}_{n+1}$, and $k(G,n)$ denote the big Ramsey degree of $G$ in $\mathbb{H}_{n+1}$. We prove that for any computable coloring $\chi$ of the copies of $G$ in $\mathbb{H}_{n+1}$, there is a copy $\mathbb{H}'$ of $\mathbb{H}_{n+1}$ that is computable from $0^{(2\delta(G,n)-1)}$ in which $\chi$ takes no more than $k(G,n)$ colors, where $\delta(G,n)$ denotes the maximum number of levels of a diary for $G$ in $\mathbb{H}_{n+1}$ (this is a finite number). It follows that the statement, "Henson graphs have finite big Ramsey degrees," is provable in ACA$_0'$. Combining this with a recent result of Cholak, Dobrinen, and McCoy [CDM] yields the equivalence of the statement with ACA$_0'$ over RCA$_0$.

**What it claims to prove.**  
Henson graphs are the countably many universal homogeneous $K_{n+1}$-free graphs, generalizing the Rado graph. Dobrinen and collaborators established over the last decade that these graphs have finite big Ramsey degrees: every finite coloring of the copies of a finite subgraph $G$ can be reduced, on some isomorphic copy of the Henson graph, to at most $k(G,n)$ colors. This paper pins down exactly how much set-existence strength second-order arithmetic needs to prove that theorem, showing it is provable in ACA$_0'$ (arithmetic comprehension together with closure under the $\omega$-th Turing jump) via an explicit, jump-computable bound on the required homogeneous copy. Combined with a companion lower-bound result of Cholak, Dobrinen, and McCoy, this yields a full reverse-mathematics equivalence: "Henson graphs have finite big Ramsey degrees" is equivalent to ACA$_0'$ over the base system RCA$_0$.

**Significance.**  
Big Ramsey degrees sit at the intersection of structural Ramsey theory, topological dynamics, and computability theory, and Henson graphs are, after the Rado graph, the paradigmatic testbed for the theory largely developed by Dobrinen over the past ten years. Calibrating the exact reverse-mathematics strength of a combinatorial theorem is a well-established and highly valued way of "explaining" a result, in the tradition of classical strength calibrations of Ramsey's theorem for pairs. Pinning an exact equivalence, rather than just an upper bound, for such a technically involved theorem is a genuine and complete contribution, though it targets a narrow, highly specialized audience and does not itself produce new Ramsey-theoretic combinatorics.

**Rating: B**

### 2. Non-Uniqueness for Nonlinear Fokker–Planck Equations and Their Associated Distribution-Dependent SDEs

**Authors:** Huaxiang Lü  
**arXiv:** [2606.31500](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31500)

**Abstract.**  
> In this paper, we study distribution-dependent stochastic differential equations on the domain $\mathcal O=\mathbb T^d$ or $\mathbb R^d$, $d\geq 2$, of the form
> $${\rm d}X_t = v(t,X_t,\rho_t)\,{\rm d}t + \sqrt{2}\, \sigma(t,X_t,\rho_t)\,{\rm d}W_t, \qquad \rho_t:=\frac{{\rm d}\mu_t}{{\rm d}x},$$
> where $\mu_t=\operatorname{Law}(X_t)$. Our main construction is carried out at the level of the associated nonlinear Fokker–Planck equations. We first build non-unique probability solutions to these PDEs and then use the superposition principle to obtain non-unique martingale solutions to the corresponding DDSDEs.
> We establish two main non-uniqueness results concerning stationary states, both on the torus and in the whole space, under the corresponding structural assumptions. First, we construct a divergence-free drift $v\in C_tL^{d-}$ such that the DDSDE admits infinitely many distinct solutions starting from the stationary initial density. This result lies at the natural critical regularity threshold: in several models, well-posedness is expected for drifts in $C_tL^{d+}$. Second, for $d\geq 3$ and every prescribed $N\in\mathbb{N}$, we construct a divergence-free drift for which the DDSDE admits at least $N$ distinct stationary martingale solutions. The resulting multiplicity of equilibrium states is reminiscent of multistability and phase-transition phenomena in physical systems.

**What it claims to prove.**  
For distribution-dependent (McKean–Vlasov-type) SDEs whose drift and diffusion depend on the law of the solution through its density, the paper exhibits explicit divergence-free drifts, right at the borderline regularity threshold $C_tL^{d-}$ (just below the critical exponent $C_tL^{d+}$ where uniqueness is expected), for which the associated nonlinear Fokker–Planck equation has infinitely many probability-density solutions from the same stationary initial state, and correspondingly the SDE has infinitely many martingale solutions. It further constructs, for any prescribed $N$, drifts producing at least $N$ distinct stationary solutions, mirroring multistability phenomena from statistical physics.

**Significance.**  
Sharp well-posedness thresholds for SDEs and their nonlinear Fokker–Planck counterparts under rough drifts have been an active theme since the DiPerna–Lions/Krylov–Röckner theory and its distribution-dependent extensions, with critical-regularity non-uniqueness constructions playing a role parallel to convex-integration non-uniqueness in fluid dynamics. Exhibiting genuine non-uniqueness exactly at the conjectured critical exponent is a natural and useful confirmation that the known well-posedness thresholds in this line of work are sharp, but the paper is a single-author construction addressing one specific model class rather than a new general theory or the resolution of a long-standing conjecture, making it a solid, well-executed technical contribution rather than a landmark one.

**Rating: C**

### 3. Tamagawa ratios and unbounded Selmer moments

**Authors:** Peter Koymans, Alexander Smith  
**arXiv:** [2606.31649](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31649)

**Abstract.**  
> We develop a framework to predict whether a family of Selmer groups has average size that is bounded or unbounded. Applying this framework to certain geometric families of abelian varieties over $\mathbb{Q}$, we give a conjectural characterization of which such families have $\ell$-Selmer groups of unbounded average size for a given prime $\ell$. In the case that the $\ell$-torsion Galois module is constant across the family, we show that our characterization is correct.
> The key tool of our technique is the Greenberg–Wiles' formula, which expresses the ratio of the sizes of a Selmer group and the corresponding dual Selmer group as a product of local factors. This formula gives a purely local lower bound for the size of a Selmer group that we conjecture is close to sharp most of the time.

**What it claims to prove.**  
Arithmetic statistics predicts, and in many cases (elliptic curves ordered by height, via Bhargava–Shankar and successors) proves, that average sizes of $\ell$-Selmer groups across natural families are finite constants. This paper studies the converse question of when the average is instead unbounded, building a general framework — based on the Greenberg–Wiles formula relating Selmer group size to a product of local Tamagawa-type ratios — for predicting boundedness versus unboundedness across families of abelian varieties. The authors give a conjectural characterization of which geometric families have unbounded $\ell$-Selmer moments and prove it whenever the $\ell$-torsion Galois representation is constant across the family.

**Significance.**  
The distribution of Selmer groups underlies essentially all modern quantitative approaches to the Birch–Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture and to bounding average ranks of elliptic curves and abelian varieties, a program driven over the last fifteen years chiefly by Bhargava, Shankar, and, more recently, by Alexander Smith's own work on the distribution of 2-Selmer groups. Identifying exactly when Selmer moments diverge, rather than assuming boundedness as a standing hypothesis, fills a genuine gap in the general theory and could reshape how families are selected for average-rank computations. The result is partly conjectural — the general characterization is proved only under a constant-torsion hypothesis — but the local, Tamagawa-ratio-based tool and the authors' strong track record suggest this is an important frame-setting contribution to arithmetic statistics rather than a fully closed structural theorem.

**Rating: B**

### 4. On clique-to-clique densities

**Authors:** Jie Ma, Tianhen Wang, Tianming Zhu  
**arXiv:** [2606.31967](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31967)

**Abstract.**  
> Let $k_r(G)$ denote the number of $r$-cliques in a graph $G$ and let $F_r(\cdot)$ be the Lovász–Simonovits $r$-clique density function. For any integers $2\le s<t$, we determine the asymptotically sharp lower bound on $k_t(G)$ in an $n$-vertex graph $G$ with a prescribed number $k_s(G)$, by showing that
> $$\frac{k_t(G)}{n^t}\ge F_t\!\left(F_s^{-1}\!\left(\frac{k_s(G)}{n^s}\right)\right),$$
> where $F_s^{-1}$ denotes the generalized inverse. This strengthens Bollobás's piecewise-linear interpolation bound and, in the case $s=2$, recovers Reiher's clique density theorem via a new inductive proof.

**What it claims to prove.**  
Given the number of $s$-cliques in an $n$-vertex graph, the paper determines the exact asymptotic minimum possible number of $t$-cliques, for every pair $2 \le s < t$, expressing the sharp bound via the composition $F_t \circ F_s^{-1}$ of Lovász–Simonovits clique density functions. This simultaneously strengthens an older piecewise-linear bound of Bollobás and, in the base case $s=2$, gives a new, purely inductive proof of Reiher's clique density theorem, originally proved via flag algebras.

**Significance.**  
The clique density theorem — conjectured by Lovász and Simonovits in 1983 and proved by Reiher in 2016 using flag-algebra semidefinite programming — is one of the signature achievements of modern extremal graph theory, pinning down the exact minimum clique density forced by a given edge density. This paper both generalizes the theorem to arbitrary pairs of clique orders and gives a new, more elementary inductive proof avoiding flag algebras, which is valuable in its own right since flag-algebra proofs are often opaque and hard to extend. Jie Ma is an established leader in extremal graph theory, and clean generalizations plus alternative proofs of landmark extremal results tend to open the door to further extensions; this is a strong, technically accomplished paper, though it builds on and re-derives a known theorem rather than resolving a new open problem from scratch.

**Rating: B**

### 5. The sharp diagonal spectral correlation inequality on the discrete cube

**Authors:** Fan Chang, Hong Liu, Miao Liu  
**arXiv:** [2606.32024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.32024)

**Abstract.**  
> We prove the sharp diagonal spectral correlation conjecture of Friedgut, Kahn, Kalai and Keller, proposed in their Fourier-analytic approach to Chvátal's conjecture. For every pair of increasing Boolean functions $f,g:\{0,1\}^n\to\{0,1\}$,
> $$\mathrm{Cov}(f,g)\ge4\sum_{\varnothing\ne S\subseteq[n]}|S|\hat{f}(S)^2\hat{g}(S)^2.$$
> Thus covariance controls the degree-weighted collision of the two nonconstant Fourier spectra, giving a sharp Fourier strengthening of the Harris–Kleitman inequality. The theorem also implies the unweighted diagonal conjecture of Friedgut–Kahn–Kalai–Keller for an increasing family and a maximal intersecting family.
> The factor $4$ is optimal, and we determine all equality cases. Apart from pairs whose relevant coordinate sets are disjoint, equality occurs only for a common dictatorship and, up to relabelling coordinates and interchanging $f$ and $g$, for the two-coordinate AND-OR pair $(f,g)=(x_i x_j,\,x_i\vee x_j)$.
> The main novelty is a correlated four-restriction induction and a sharp endpoint convolution inequality. The usual two-restriction induction behind Harris–Kleitman sees only the parallel restricted pairs and loses the mixed Fourier information needed to control the degree-weighted diagonal spectral energy. We instead couple the four codimension-one restricted pairs with correlation $1/2$; this precise correlation extracts the missing degree-weighted energy as a nonnegative square.

**What it claims to prove.**  
For increasing Boolean functions $f,g$ on the discrete cube, the paper proves a sharp lower bound on their covariance in terms of a degree-weighted sum of products of their Fourier coefficients, with optimal constant 4 and a full classification of equality cases. This confirms the "sharp diagonal spectral correlation conjecture" that Friedgut, Kahn, Kalai, and Keller introduced as part of a Fourier-analytic strategy toward Chvátal's conjecture, and as a corollary it also establishes their weaker unweighted diagonal conjecture for increasing families and maximal intersecting families. The key technical innovation is a "four-restriction" induction — coupling four codimension-one restrictions with correlation $1/2$ — that extracts strictly more Fourier information than the classical two-restriction argument behind the Harris–Kleitman inequality.

**Significance.**  
Chvátal's conjecture (1974), asserting that the largest intersecting family within any downward-closed family of sets can always be taken to be a star, is one of the oldest and most stubborn open problems in extremal set theory, and the Friedgut–Kahn–Kalai–Keller program is among the more promising recent analytic approaches to it, building on the Fourier-analytic toolkit for Boolean functions developed by Kahn–Kalai–Linial, Friedgut, and others. Resolving their sharp diagonal spectral correlation conjecture — with an optimal constant and complete equality analysis — is a genuine advance in this program and a sharp strengthening of the classical Harris–Kleitman correlation inequality, a staple tool across combinatorics and probability. Hong Liu is a leading extremal combinatorialist, and the new four-restriction induction technique is likely to be reusable elsewhere in the analysis of Boolean functions. The result stops short of resolving Chvátal's conjecture itself, which remains open, but it is a technically striking and sharply optimal piece of progress on one of the field's most famous problems.

**Rating: A**
