### 1. Enumerating Matrices with Prescribed Entries in an Adjoint Orbit

**Authors:** Samrith Ram  
**arXiv:** [2606.27497](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27497)

**What it claims to prove.**  
For $n \times n$ matrices over a finite field $\mathbb{F}_q$, the paper studies intersections of conjugacy classes (adjoint orbits) with affine coordinate subspaces — i.e., matrices with a fixed Jordan type and prescribed entries. The main result treats the case of prescribed columns: for a partially defined linear map $\mathbb{F}_q^r \to \mathbb{F}_q^n$, the paper gives a Hall scalar product formula for the number of extensions to a full endomorphism of $\mathbb{F}_q^n$ with prescribed similarity invariants. The formula is expressed in terms of skew modified Hall–Littlewood functions and $q$-Whittaker functions. Applications include counting monic matrix polynomials over $\mathbb{F}_q$ with prescribed Smith normal form and prescribed determinant, and recovering the Gerstenhaber–Reiner formula for the number of square matrices with a fixed characteristic polynomial. The paper also observes that known point-count formulas for Hessenberg varieties yield related formulas for Hessenberg supports involving chromatic quasisymmetric functions.

**Significance.**  
Counting matrices over finite fields with prescribed properties is a classical problem connecting representation theory, algebraic geometry, and symmetric function theory. The Hall–Littlewood functions and $q$-Whittaker functions appearing in the formula are central to algebraic combinatorics, the theory of Macdonald polynomials, and the Langlands program. The Gerstenhaber–Reiner formula — giving the number of $n \times n$ matrices over $\mathbb{F}_q$ with a fixed characteristic polynomial — is a foundational classical result, and its recovery as a special case provides strong validation of the general framework. More striking is the connection to Hessenberg varieties and chromatic quasisymmetric functions: the latter have been at the center of intense recent activity around the Stanley–Stembridge conjecture, and a bridge between Hessenberg point counts and the present adjoint-orbit enumeration is a substantive new observation that is likely to stimulate further work in both directions.

### 2. A Combinatorial Proof for the Positivity of the Normalized Jacobi Triple Product Tails

**Authors:** Xiangyu Ding, Lisa Hui Sun  
**arXiv:** [2606.27507](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27507)

**What it claims to prove.**  
For $k \geq 1$, the paper proves that all coefficients $[q^n z^s]\,J_k(z,q)$ are nonneg, where $J_k(z,q)$ is the normalized Jacobi triple product tail defined by
$$J_k(z,q) = \frac{\sum_{j=k}^{\infty}(-1)^{j-k}q^{\binom{j+1}{2}}(z^{-j}+\cdots+z^j)}{(zq,q/z;q)_\infty}.$$
This establishes Merca's stronger nonnegativity conjecture on truncated Jacobi triple product series in full generality, and yields infinite families of linear inequalities for two-colored partitions and partitions with parts in residue classes $\pm S \pmod{R}$. The proof is purely combinatorial: a sign-reversing involution reduces the tails to invariant subsets indexed by the generalized minimal-excludant of partitions, and then an injection between consecutive invariant subsets is constructed by combining an invertible lift operator on Frobenius arms with Konan's size-and-length-preserving bijection.

**Significance.**  
The Jacobi triple product identity is one of the cornerstones of partition theory and $q$-series, with deep connections to modular forms, representation theory, and mathematical physics. Truncated versions of classical $q$-series identities and their positivity properties have attracted sustained attention since the work of Andrews and Merca around 2012. Merca's conjecture — that every coefficient of the normalized Jacobi triple product tail is nonneg — is a natural and concrete prediction that had been open for over a decade. Its proof in full generality is a definitive resolution. Beyond the statement itself, the combinatorial method is notable: the sign-reversing involution and the lift-operator construction avoid analytic $q$-series machinery entirely, placing the result squarely within the tradition of combinatorial proofs of $q$-series identities inaugurated by Sylvester and Dyson. The new objects introduced (generalized minimal-excludant, invertible lift operator on Frobenius arms) are likely to find further applications in partition inequalities.

### 3. The Erdős $n^2/25$ Max-Cut Conjecture for Small Multiples of Five, via a Per-Root-MaxCut Envelope and Blow-Up Integrality

**Authors:** Alper Ferudun  
**arXiv:** [2606.28041](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28041)

**What it claims to prove.**  
Erdős conjectured that every triangle-free graph on $N$ vertices can be made bipartite by deleting at most $N^2/25$ edges, with the bound attained by the balanced blow-up $C_5[N/5]$. Writing $a(N)$ for the maximum bipartite edit distance over all triangle-free $N$-vertex graphs, the paper proves $a(5n) = n^2$ for every $1 \leq n \leq 40$, i.e., for all $N \in \{5, 10, \ldots, 200\}$. The proof is computer-assisted and combines three ingredients: (i) a *per-root-MaxCut envelope* $U_7(W)$ satisfying $d_{\rm mono}(W) \leq U_7(W)$ for every triangle-free graphon $W$, with equality at the $C_5$ blow-up; (ii) an order-10 flag-algebra certificate — per-root-MaxCut rows at 7 and 8 roots together with rooted-Horn cuts and a manifestly positive-semidefinite moment block — bounding $U_7(W) \leq \frac{2}{25} + \delta$ with explicit rational $\delta \approx 4.856 \times 10^{-5}$ for all triangle-free graphons of edge density in $[0.2486, 0.3197]$; and (iii) the blow-up identity $\beta(G[t]) = t^2\,\beta(G)$ combined with the integrality of $\beta$, which converts the graphon bound into $\beta(G) \leq n^2$ for $N = 5n$ and $n \leq 40$. The paper also identifies a self-tight obstruction that prevents the same approach from resolving all $n$, and explains precisely why the envelope at orders 9 and 10 fails to reach the constant needed for larger $n$.

**Significance.**  
The Erdős triangle-free bipartite edit conjecture is among the most prominent open problems in extremal graph theory. It generalises the classical Rademacher–Erdős theorem on triangles in dense graphs and is equivalent to a sharp statement about the structure of triangle-free graphs near the $C_5$ blow-up extremum. The best prior unconditional result (Balogh–Clemen–Lidický) proved the conjecture in the two extreme density tails — edge density below $0.2486$ or above $0.3197$ — but left the medium-density band open. The present paper closes the gap for all multiples of 5 up to 200 by combining flag-algebra semidefinite programming (with exact rational Gram factorization, cross-checked against brute-force max-cut on all triangle-free graphs of order $\leq 12$) with a new analytic object, the per-root-MaxCut envelope, which is tight at the extremal $C_5$ blow-up. The envelope and the blow-up integrality argument are likely to be reusable tools in further attacks on the conjecture.

### 4. A Unit-Distance Graph in the Plane with Independence Ratio Below $1/4$

**Authors:** Ákos Dücz, Dániel Varga  
**arXiv:** [2606.28157](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28157)

**What it claims to prove.**  
The paper proves that there exists a finite unit-distance graph in the plane with independence ratio strictly smaller than $1/4$, answering a question of Erdős. Equivalently, it establishes that the fractional chromatic number of the plane satisfies $\chi_f(\mathbb{R}^2) > 4$. The proof follows the framework of Matolcsi, Ruzsa, Varga, and Zsámboki — based on the geometric fractional chromatic number and fractional coloring of Cayley graphs of $\mathbb{R}^2$ — but augments their 27-vertex construction with a carefully chosen two-vertex addition that pushes the geometric fractional chromatic number from exactly 4 to a value strictly greater than 4. This simultaneously disproves Conjecture 1 of Matolcsi–Ruzsa–Varga–Zsámboki and yields the strict lower bound $\chi_f(\mathbb{R}^2) > 4$. The construction is in principle fully explicit, though the resulting graph has an enormous number of vertices.

**Significance.**  
The chromatic number of the plane (the Hadwiger–Nelson problem, 1950) — how many colors suffice to color $\mathbb{R}^2$ so that no two unit-distance points share a color — is one of the most celebrated and long-standing open problems in combinatorics and discrete geometry, with the answer known only to lie in $\{4, 5, 6, 7\}$. De Grey's 2018 breakthrough establishing the lower bound 5 came via a finite unit-distance graph with independence ratio below $1/4$, sparking renewed global interest. The fractional chromatic number $\chi_f(\mathbb{R}^2)$ is a natural relaxation satisfying $\chi_f(\mathbb{R}^2) \leq \chi(\mathbb{R}^2)$, and the previous best lower bound was $\chi_f(\mathbb{R}^2) \geq 4$ (matching the integer lower bound of 4 known since Moser–Moser). Pushing this to the strict inequality $\chi_f(\mathbb{R}^2) > 4$ is a meaningful advance in its own right, and any further improvement of the lower bound on $\chi_f(\mathbb{R}^2)$ toward 5 would approach the integer chromatic number from below. Refuting Conjecture 1 of Matolcsi–Ruzsa–Varga–Zsámboki also clears away a false belief that had guided the area and opens the door to new constructions.

### 5. All Limit Points of the Largest Roots of Matching Polynomials Are Determined

**Authors:** Zhaoxi Li, Shi-Mei Ma, Jianfeng Wang, Weifan Wang  
**arXiv:** [2606.28162](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28162)

**What it claims to prove.**  
Let $\mu(G)$ denote the largest root of the matching polynomial of a graph $G$. The paper gives a complete characterization of the set of limit points of $\{\mu(G) : G \text{ a graph}\}$. Precisely, for any $\gamma \geq \tau^{1/2} + \tau^{-1/2}$ — where $\tau = (\sqrt{5}+1)/2$ is the golden ratio — there exists a graph sequence $\{G_i\}$ with $\mu(G_i) \to \gamma$, so every such $\gamma$ is a limit point. For $\gamma < \tau^{1/2} + \tau^{-1/2}$, all limit points of the largest matching roots are explicitly identified. Together these two parts fully settle the structure of the limit point set, providing the complete analogue for matching polynomials of the classical Hoffman–Schwenk theorem on limit points of spectral radii of graphs.

**Significance.**  
The matching polynomial of a graph — whose coefficients count matchings by size — is a central object in graph theory and combinatorial statistical mechanics: its roots govern the radius of convergence of the hard-core partition function, and the location of its zeros is connected to phase transitions in the monomer-dimer model. The theory of limit points of graph eigenvalues was initiated by Hoffman and Schwenk in 1972 for the adjacency spectrum and subsequently extended to other graph polynomials and matrices, but the matching polynomial case had remained open. The threshold value $\tau^{1/2} + \tau^{-1/2}$ (where $\tau$ is the golden ratio) is entirely natural: it is the matching root of the infinite path graph $P_\infty$, and its appearance as the critical threshold mirrors the role of the golden ratio in spectral graph theory for path-like structures. The completeness of the characterization — distinguishing the discrete structure below the threshold from the continuum above it — makes this a clean, definitive result in the tradition of classical spectral combinatorics.
