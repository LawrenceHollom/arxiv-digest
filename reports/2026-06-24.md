### 1. Coloring sparse random Cayley graphs

**Authors:** Nathan Tung  
**arXiv:** [2606.23762](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23762)

**What it claims to prove.**  
For any finite abelian group $Z$, the Cayley graph on $Z$ generated by $c\log|Z|$ uniformly random elements is properly 3-colorable with high probability as $|Z| \to \infty$, for some absolute constant $c > 0$. This bound is asymptotically tight. It improves the previously best-known bound of Alon, which required $\frac{1}{4}\log\log|Z|$ generators for 3-colorability, by an exponential factor. The paper also makes progress toward Alon's suggestion that a bound of $c\log|G|$ generators should suffice for any finite solvable group $G$.

**Significance.**  
The chromatic number of Cayley graphs on finite groups is a fundamental problem connecting additive combinatorics, group theory, and probabilistic graph theory. Alon's $\frac{1}{4}\log\log|Z|$ bound had stood as the best-known threshold for years; the new result moves this to $\Theta(\log|Z|)$, a qualitative improvement demonstrating that the genuine threshold is logarithmic in the group order rather than iterated logarithmic. The tightness of the new bound is especially striking, as it exactly pins down the order of magnitude of the threshold and shows that the transition to 3-colorability occurs at logarithmic scale. The progress toward solvable groups connects the problem to deeper questions in group theory and the algebraic structure of Cayley graphs, and positions this result as a significant advance in the theory of random algebraic graphs.

### 2. Asymptotics of the Average Stack-Sorting Depth

**Authors:** Jerry Zhang  
**arXiv:** [2606.24110](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24110)

**What it claims to prove.**  
Let $\mathcal{D}_n$ denote the average number of passes of West's stack-sorting map $s$ required to sort a uniformly random permutation of $[n]$. Using the framework of stack-sorting diagrams and tableaux introduced by Defant, the paper proves that the limit $\lim_{n\to\infty} \mathcal{D}_n/n$ exists. This resolves a conjecture of West originally proposed in 1990. As a byproduct, the paper constructs a monotonically increasing sequence converging to this limit, improving upon Defant's previously known lower bound of $\lambda \approx 0.62433$.

**Significance.**  
West introduced his stack-sorting conjecture in 1990, making this a 36-year-old open problem in enumerative combinatorics. Stack-sorting, originating in Knuth's analysis of permutations sortable by a stack, has been studied intensively by West and especially by Defant, who has proved a wide range of structural and enumerative results about the operation. The existence of the limit $\lim \mathcal{D}_n/n$ is the most fundamental asymptotic question about the average behavior of the stack-sorting map, and its confirmation gives a clean linear growth law for the expected sorting depth. The proof exploits Defant's framework of diagrams and tableaux — showing that this framework is powerful enough to resolve the conjecture that motivated much of its development — and the construction of a monotone approximating sequence gives quantitative control over the limit that was not previously available.

### 3. New Tower-Type Lower Bounds for Hypergraph Ramsey Numbers

**Authors:** Hanzhi Bai, Longma Du, Xinyu Hu, Ruilong Liu, Guanghui Wang  
**arXiv:** [2606.24198](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24198)

**What it claims to prove.**  
The $k$-uniform hypergraph Ramsey number $r_k(s,m)$ is the least $N$ such that every red/blue coloring of the $k$-subsets of $[N]$ contains either a red $s$-set or a blue $m$-set. For the diagonal case $r_k(k+1,k+1)$, the stepping-up lemma does not apply, making tower-type lower bounds especially difficult. The previous best, due to Pudlák, Rödl, and Wesley, was $r_k(k+1,k+1) \geq s_3(\lfloor k/4\rfloor) \geq 4\operatorname{twr}_{\lfloor k/4\rfloor-4}(2)$, where $\operatorname{twr}$ denotes the tower function. This paper, for $k \geq 6$, improves this to $r_k(k+1,k+1) > s_3(\lfloor k/2\rfloor - 2)$ by overcoming an obstruction in the Pudlák–Rödl–Wesley construction, roughly doubling the tower height. It also gives an exact characterization of the three-color shift number $s_3(k)$ and, for $k \geq 5$, the new bound $s_3(k) \geq (\operatorname{twr}_{k-2}(2))^2$, improving Pudlák and Rödl's bound. Consequently, for $k \geq 14$, $r_k(k+1,k+1) > (\operatorname{twr}_{\lfloor k/2\rfloor-4}(2))^2$.

**Significance.**  
Off-diagonal hypergraph Ramsey numbers $r_k(s,m)$ with fixed $s$ and growing $m$ are governed by the stepping-up lemma of Erdős, Hajnal, and Rado, which yields towers of height $k-2$. The diagonal case $r_k(k+1,k+1)$ is the hardest regime: stepping-up does not apply, and Fox singled it out as an important target for tower-type bounds. Pudlák, Rödl, and Wesley provided the first tower-type lower bounds; this paper doubles the tower height by identifying and circumventing the obstruction that limited their approach. The exact characterization of $s_3(k)$ and the squared-tower form of the improved bounds are additional independent contributions of intrinsic interest to Ramsey theorists. Guanghui Wang is a prominent combinatorialist at Shandong University with substantial contributions to extremal graph and hypergraph theory.

### 4. Structural Reductions for Monochromatic Matchings and Ramsey Tilings

**Authors:** Hong Liu, Maksim Turevskii, Lanchao Wang, Zhifei Yan  
**arXiv:** [2606.24863](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24863)

**What it claims to prove.**  
The paper develops a topology-free structural framework for monochromatic matchings and tiling problems in edge-colored hypergraphs, with two main theorems. First, a structural reduction: every $r$-edge-coloring of a sufficiently pseudorandom $t$-uniform hypergraph can be reduced, with $o(n)$ loss in the largest monochromatic matching, to a coloring of $K_n^{(t)}$ whose vertex set is partitioned into at most $r$ parts and whose edge colors depend only on intersection profiles. The proof combines hypergraph regularity, LP duality, and convex-geometric compression. As corollaries the paper obtains a topology-free proof of the asymptotic Alon–Frankl–Lovász (AFL) theorem, a sparse random transference theorem, and near-exact bounds in a linear-uniformity regime of Meunier's stable Kneser conjecture. Second, for any graph $H$ and integer $r$, it proves $Rt_r(H;K_n) = (\beta_{r,H}+o(1))n$, where $Rt_r(H;K_n)$ is the minimum over all $r$-edge-colorings of $K_n$ of the largest monochromatic $H$-tiling, and $\beta_{r,H}$ is effectively computable from finitely many linear programs depending only on $H$ and $r$. This gives an asymptotic solution to the complete-host multicolour Ramsey-tiling problem, extending the classical two-colour theorem of Burr, Erdős, and Spencer to all graphs and all numbers of colours.

**Significance.**  
The Alon–Frankl–Lovász theorem (1986) determines the chromatic number of Kneser hypergraphs and equivalently identifies the sharp minimum monochromatic matching size in every $r$-coloring of $K_n^{(t)}$. Until now, every known proof of this theorem depended on topological methods — Borsuk–Ulam or its extensions — and the absence of a combinatorial proof has been a major conceptual gap. The topology-free approach in this paper is not merely a reproof: by replacing topology with regularity and linear programming, it opens the theorem to sparse transference, random settings, and effective computation in ways that topological proofs cannot. The asymptotic Ramsey-tiling theorem is a qualitatively distinct contribution: determining $Rt_r(H;K_n)$ up to $o(n)$ for every $H$ and $r$ simultaneously, via a uniform finite LP computation, is a remarkably complete result. Hong Liu is one of the most prominent extremal combinatorialists of his generation, with an outstanding record of resolving major open problems in Ramsey theory, graph coloring, and combinatorial structure.

### 5. A Resolution of Erdős Problems 593 and 1177: Obligatory Triple Systems and Exact Spectra

**Authors:** Eric Li (Trinity College, University of Cambridge)  
**arXiv:** [2606.24882](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24882)

**What it claims to prove.**  
The paper resolves two problems from Erdős's problem list. Problem 593 asks which finite triple systems are obligatory — meaning they appear as a subhypergraph of every uncountably chromatic triple system. The paper proves the exact characterization: after isolated vertices are removed, a finite triple system is obligatory if and only if it is linear, every hyperedge-node of its Levi graph has an incident bridge, and every Berge cycle is even. Equivalently, the class of obligatory triple systems is exactly that generated from private-vertex expansions of finite bipartite graphs by finite disjoint unions and one-point amalgamations. The proof introduces an exact bridge-trace theorem for complete-rank one-apex sequence lifts. The paper also proves that for every uncountable cardinal $\kappa$ there exists a linear triple system of chromatic number exactly $\kappa$, with at most $2^{2^\mu}$ vertices when $\kappa = \mu^+$. Combining these, the paper derives a class-valued exact avoidance-spectrum dichotomy for every finite forbidden triple system, and in particular settles Problem 1177 with the answer "yes, no, and yes."

**Significance.**  
Erdős's problems on obligatory subgraphs in uncountably chromatic graphs and hypergraphs lie at the interface of combinatorics and combinatorial set theory, connecting the finite and infinite aspects of graph coloring. The characterization of obligatory triple systems is a deep structural theorem: it gives a complete, explicit, and verifiable criterion for a finite triple system to be unavoidable in any uncountably chromatic context, and the answer — bipartite Levi graph, bridge at every hyperedge-node, no odd Berge cycles — is both elegant and nontrivial to establish. The exact spectrum result (every uncountable cardinal is realized as the chromatic number of some linear triple system) completes the picture for triple systems and is a meaningful contribution to infinite combinatorics. Resolving two distinct Erdős problems in a single paper, together with a general avoidance-spectrum dichotomy, represents a substantial advance in the structural theory of infinite hypergraphs.
