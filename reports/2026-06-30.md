### 1. An Improved Upper Bound for the Bilu–Linial Conjecture via Interlacing Families

**Authors:** Zhiqiang Xu, Xinyue Zhang  
**arXiv:** [2606.28797](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28797)

**What it claims to prove.**  
The Bilu–Linial conjecture asserts that every $d$-regular graph admits a signing $\sigma$ of its edges such that the spectral radius of the signed adjacency matrix satisfies $\rho(A_\sigma) \le 2\sqrt{d-1}$ — the Ramanujan bound. Bilu and Linial themselves proved the weaker bound $O(\sqrt{d\log^3 d})$ for graphs of maximum degree $d$, and Marcus, Spielman, and Srivastava (MSS) later confirmed the conjecture exactly in the bipartite case. This paper proves that every graph of maximum degree $d$ admits a signing $\sigma$ with $\rho(A_\sigma) \le 2\sqrt{3(d-1)}$, removing the polylogarithmic factor from Bilu and Linial's original estimate and replacing it with an explicit constant-factor loss. The proof extends the method of interlacing polynomials introduced by MSS, combined with results on mixed characteristic polynomials due to MSS and to Bownik.

**Significance.**  
The Bilu–Linial conjecture, motivated by an inductive scheme for constructing Ramanujan graphs (graphs with optimal spectral expansion), has been a central target in spectral graph theory since 2006. The interlacing-polynomial technology used here is the same machinery that powered MSS's celebrated resolution of the Kadison–Singer problem and their construction of bipartite Ramanujan graphs of every degree; extending it to remove the logarithmic slack in the general (non-bipartite) case is a substantive technical advance, bringing the best known bound to within a small constant factor of the conjectured truth for the first time in nearly two decades. Because the conjecture's inductive signing scheme would yield new constructions of near-Ramanujan expanders if fully resolved, sharpening the constant is directly relevant to explicit expander constructions, not merely of theoretical interest.

### 2. A Sharp 5/8 Bound for an Erdős–Sós Pairwise-Sums Problem

**Authors:** Ricky Cipollini  
**arXiv:** [2606.29361](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29361)

**What it claims to prove.**  
Let $f_3(N)$ be the least integer such that every set $A \subseteq \{1,\ldots,N\}$ of size at least $f_3(N)$ must contain three distinct elements $a,b,c \in A$ with $a+b, a+c, b+c$ all also in $A$. The paper proves $f_3(N) \le 5N/8 + O(1)$. Combined with the known extremal construction $[N/8,N/4] \cup [N/2,N]$ (which avoids the configuration and has size close to $5N/8$), this pins down $f_3(N) = 5N/8 + O(1)$ exactly, resolving Erdős Problem 865 in full. The proof is self-contained, and an earlier conditional version of the key reduction step has additionally been formalized and machine-checked in Lean 4/Mathlib.

**Significance.**  
Erdős Problem 865 belongs to the actively curated list of open problems originally posed by Paul Erdős (now maintained and tracked online), which continues to attract steady attention from the combinatorics community decades after Erdős's death. Determining $f_3(N)$ exactly — matching a clean construction with a matching upper bound of $5N/8$ — gives a complete and aesthetically satisfying answer to a natural Schur-type "pairwise sums" question in the spirit of Erdős and Sós's work on sum-closed configurations in $[N]$. The accompanying Lean formalization of part of the argument is also notable: it reflects a growing trend, championed in recent years by figures such as Terence Tao, of using proof assistants to verify intricate finite combinatorial arguments, adding an extra layer of confidence to a result that closes a long-standing entry on Erdős's list.

### 3. On a Problem of Sivaraman and a Problem of Gyárfás

**Authors:** Kaiyang Lan, Wenlong Zhong  
**arXiv:** [2606.29873](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29873)

**What it claims to prove.**  
Sivaraman (2020) asked for the optimal $\chi$-bounding function for the class of graphs whose complements have girth at least $6$. Writing $F(s) = \max\{\chi(G) : \omega(G) \le s,\ \mathrm{g}(\overline{G}) \ge 6\}$, the paper proves $c(s/\log s)^{4/3} \le F(s) \le (1+o(1)) s^{3/2}/\log s$ for an absolute constant $c>0$, and determines $F(s)$ exactly for $s \le 6$ (e.g. $F(6)=8$), with all small-case bounds shown sharp. Separately, calling a graph $G$ *almost perfect* if $\alpha(H)\omega(H)+1 \ge |V(H)|$ for every induced subgraph $H$, Gyárfás (2023) asked whether almost perfect graphs are $\chi$-bounded by $g(x)=x+1$. The paper answers this negatively, exhibiting that no constant $c$ makes $\chi(G) \le \omega(G)+c$ hold for every almost perfect graph.

**Significance.**  
$\chi$-boundedness — whether bounding the clique number of a hereditary graph class forces a bound on the chromatic number — has been one of the most active areas of structural graph theory in the past decade, propelled by progress on the Gyárfás–Sumner conjecture and related questions about forbidden induced subgraphs. Sivaraman's question sits in this tradition, targeting graphs whose complements avoid short cycles, a natural relaxation of perfection; pinning the optimal exponent down to within a $\log s$ factor (an essentially polynomial-order resolution) is a strong quantitative achievement. The disproof of Gyárfás's almost-perfect conjecture is perhaps the more striking result: "almost perfect" graphs satisfy a local inequality ($\alpha\omega + 1 \ge |V|$) that mimics the defining property of perfect graphs, so the natural expectation — by analogy with the perfect graph theorem — was a tight additive $\chi \le \omega + 1$ bound. Showing this expectation fails for every constant $c$ corrects the intuition that has guided the area and clarifies the boundary between perfection and approximate perfection.

### 4. A Proof of Seymour's Second Neighborhood Conjecture for Oriented Graphs with Minimum Out-Degree Equal to 7

**Authors:** Arpan Sadhukhan, R. B. Sandeep, Sagnik Sen  
**arXiv:** [2606.30588](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30588)

**What it claims to prove.**  
Seymour's second neighborhood conjecture (SSNC) states that every oriented graph (a digraph with no 2-cycles) has a vertex $v$ whose second out-neighborhood is at least as large as its first out-neighborhood. The paper proves the conjecture holds for all oriented graphs with minimum out-degree exactly $7$. This extends the previously known range — minimum out-degree at most $6$, established by Kaneko and Locke in 2001 — and is, by the authors' account, the first improvement of the minimum-out-degree threshold for SSNC in two decades. The proof is partially computer-assisted: a sequence of local structural reductions narrows the problem to a finite list of obstruction configurations, each of which is then eliminated via reproducible CP-SAT infeasibility checks (using OR-Tools).

**Significance.**  
SSNC, posed by Paul Seymour around 1990, is one of the cleanest and most stubbornly open problems in digraph theory, closely connected to the Caccetta–Häggkvist conjecture on short directed cycles. It is known to hold for tournaments (Fisher 1996) and, via Kaneko–Locke, for all oriented graphs of minimum out-degree at most 6, but no further progress on the out-degree threshold had been made since 2001 despite sustained interest. Extending the threshold to 7 is a meaningful structural advance in its own right, and the methodology is arguably just as significant: reducing the infinite problem to a finite, SAT-solver-checkable obstruction set continues a tradition — from the four-color theorem to modern flag-algebra computer proofs — of combining human-guided structural reduction with exhaustive computer verification to chip away at conjectures that resist purely human case analysis. This opens a concrete, replicable path for attacking out-degree 8 and beyond.

### 5. Nearly-Uniform Degree Distributions in Spanning Subgraphs

**Authors:** Richard Montgomery, Alexey Pokrovskiy, Benny Sudakov  
**arXiv:** [2606.30612](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30612)

**What it claims to prove.**  
The paper shows that, when $d = o(n)$, every $d$-regular $n$-vertex graph contains a spanning subgraph whose degree distribution is nearly uniform: for each $0 \le i \le d$, the number of vertices of degree $i$ in the subgraph is $(1+o(1))n/(d+1)$. This confirms a conjecture of Alon and Wei on irregular subgraphs in the sparse regime, and strengthens a previous partial result of Fox, Luo, and Pham.

**Significance.**  
Questions about how evenly a graph's combinatorial structure can be "spread out" — here, finding a spanning subgraph whose degree sequence is as close to uniform as possible across $\{0,1,\ldots,d\}$ — sit at the heart of extremal and probabilistic graph theory, complementing the well-studied opposite question of constructing maximally *irregular* subgraphs. The Alon–Wei conjecture asked whether near-perfect balance across all degree values is always achievable, and this paper settles it completely in the sparse regime via a clean, complete proof. The result is authored by three of the most prominent figures in contemporary extremal and probabilistic combinatorics, whose individual and joint work includes major progress on problems such as the Ryser–Brualdi–Stein conjecture and high-girth Steiner systems; a complete resolution of a named conjecture by this group, strengthening an existing partial result, is a strong, citable addition to the literature on degree-sequence regularity, with techniques likely to transfer to related "spread-out subgraph" problems.
