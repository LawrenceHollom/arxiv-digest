### 1. An Improved Upper Bound for the Bilu-Linial Conjecture via Interlacing Families

**Authors:** Zhiqiang Xu, Xinyue Zhang  
**arXiv:** [2606.28797](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.28797)

**Abstract.**  
> The Bilu-Linial conjecture asserts that every $d$-regular graph admits a signing $\sigma$ such that the spectral radius of the signed adjacency matrix $A_\sigma$ satisfies $\rho(A_\sigma)\le 2\sqrt{d-1}$. Bilu and Linial also proved the weaker bound $O(\sqrt{d\log^3 d})$ for graphs of maximum degree $d$. Marcus, Spielman, and Srivastava confirmed the conjecture in the case of $d$-regular bipartite graphs. In this paper, we prove that every graph of maximum degree $d$ has a signing $\sigma$ such that $\rho(A_\sigma)\le 2\sqrt{3(d-1)}$. This removes the polylogarithmic factor from the estimate of Bilu and Linial and gives an explicit $2\sqrt{3(d-1)}$ two-sided spectral bound. The proof builds on the method of interlacing polynomials introduced by Marcus, Spielman, and Srivastava, together with results on mixed characteristic polynomials established by Marcus, Spielman, and Srivastava and by Bownik.

**What it claims to prove.**  
The Bilu–Linial conjecture predicts that every $d$-regular graph has a $\pm 1$ edge-signing whose signed adjacency matrix has spectral radius at most $2\sqrt{d-1}$ — the Ramanujan-graph bound. The paper proves a clean, fully explicit bound of $2\sqrt{3(d-1)}$ for *every* graph of maximum degree $d$ (not just regular graphs), eliminating the polylogarithmic loss present in Bilu and Linial's original $O(\sqrt{d\log^3 d})$ estimate. The proof extends the interlacing-polynomials machinery that Marcus, Spielman, and Srivastava used to resolve the bipartite-regular case, combined with mixed characteristic polynomial estimates.

**Significance.**  
The Bilu–Linial conjecture sits at the heart of spectral graph theory's connection to Ramanujan graphs, lifts, and the Kadison–Singer problem; the interlacing-polynomials technique that underlies it was central to Marcus–Spielman–Srivastava's celebrated resolution of Kadison–Singer. The conjecture itself remains open in general, but the constant $2\sqrt{3(d-1)} \approx 3.46\sqrt{d}$ is a marked quantitative improvement over the previous polylog-loss bound, and the extension to general bounded-degree (not just regular) graphs broadens the technique's reach. This is a solid, technically accomplished advance on a major open conjecture, executed with an established and powerful method, though it falls short of the conjecture's sharp constant $2\sqrt{d-1}$ and of a full resolution.

**Rating: B**

### 2. No involutions in the missing Moore graph

**Authors:** Yawara Ishida  
**arXiv:** [2606.29183](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29183)

**Abstract.**  
> The Moore graph of degree $57$, if one exists, is the remaining open case of the Hoffman-Singleton classification in diameter two. Although its existence remains open, substantial restrictions on the automorphism group of such a graph are known. In this paper we prove that a Moore graph of degree $57$ has no involutory automorphisms. The proof combines the known fixed-point structure of an involution with a module-theoretic obstruction. More precisely, we consider the vertex module over the ring of 2-adic integers and the direct summand given by the image of the spectral idempotent for the eigenvalue $-8$. Comparing the ordinary trace of the involution on this summand with the dimension of its Brauer quotient gives a contradiction.

**What it claims to prove.**  
A Moore graph of degree 57 and diameter 2 would have 3250 vertices and girth 5, and its existence is the last open case of the 1960 Hoffman–Singleton classification (degrees 2, 3, 7, 57 are the only possibilities; graphs are known to exist for 2, 3, 7, and the existence question for 57 remains famously open). This paper shows that if such a graph exists, its automorphism group contains no involutions (elements of order 2). The argument is algebraic: it studies the vertex permutation module over the 2-adic integers, isolates the summand corresponding to the eigenvalue $-8$ eigenspace via a spectral idempotent, and derives a numerical contradiction by comparing an ordinary trace with a Brauer-character (mod-2) computation.

**Significance.**  
The existence of a Moore graph of degree 57 is one of the oldest and most celebrated open problems in algebraic graph theory, having resisted resolution for over six decades despite an array of strong necessary conditions (it is known, for instance, that the automorphism group has order dividing certain small numbers, is not vertex-transitive in most plausible scenarios, etc.). This paper adds a genuinely new constraint — no involutions at all — which is a meaningful tightening of the very restrictive vise already squeezing the hypothetical graph's symmetry group. It does not resolve existence, and the technique (representation theory of the automorphism group acting on eigenspaces) is a continuation of a well-established line of attack rather than a structurally new idea, but incremental progress on this specific problem is always notable given its prominence and difficulty.

**Rating: C**

### 3. A sharp 5/8 bound for an Erdős-Sós pairwise-sums problem

**Authors:** Ricky Cipollini  
**arXiv:** [2606.29361](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29361)

**Abstract.**  
> Let $f_3(N)$ be the least integer such that every set $A\subseteq\{1,\ldots,N\}$ of size at least $f_3(N)$ contains distinct elements $a,b,c\in A$ such that $a+b\in A$, $a+c\in A$, and $b+c\in A$. We prove that $f_3(N)\le 5N/8+O(1)$. Together with the standard construction $[N/8,N/4]\cup[N/2,N]$, this gives $f_3(N)=5N/8+O(1)$, resolving Erdős Problem 865. The proof is self-contained. An earlier conditional version of the reduction has also been formalized in Lean 4/Mathlib with no sorries and no added axioms.

**What it claims to prove.**  
The paper pins down the exact asymptotic threshold $f_3(N) = 5N/8 + O(1)$ for the least size forcing a subset $A \subseteq \{1,\dots,N\}$ to contain three distinct elements $a,b,c$ with all three pairwise sums $a+b$, $a+c$, $b+c$ also lying in $A$. The upper bound $f_3(N) \le 5N/8 + O(1)$ is matched by the known extremal construction $[N/8,N/4]\cup[N/2,N]$, giving a sharp resolution. This settles Erdős Problem 865 from Erdős' published problem lists (as catalogued in Thomas Bloom's online Erdős Problems database).

**Significance.**  
Erdős's problem lists contain hundreds of concrete, often deceptively simple-looking questions in additive and extremal combinatorics, many of which remain open for decades; a clean, sharp resolution — matching upper and lower bounds exactly up to an additive constant — is a satisfying and complete answer of the kind the area values highly, even though the underlying combinatorial statement is narrow in scope rather than broadly structural. The result is solo-authored and the proof is reported as self-contained, with an earlier conditional reduction formally verified in Lean 4/Mathlib, reflecting the growing and welcome trend of machine-checked verification accompanying work on the Erdős problem corpus. This is a nice, complete piece of work on a specific named problem, but it does not open new general machinery or resolve a structurally central conjecture.

**Rating: C**

### 4. A proof of Seymour's second neighborhood conjecture for oriented graphs with minimum out-degree equal to 7

**Authors:** Arpan Sadhukhan, R. B. Sandeep, Sagnik Sen  
**arXiv:** [2606.30588](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30588)

**Abstract.**  
> We prove Seymour's second neighborhood conjecture on oriented graphs whose minimum out-degree is equal to $7$. This gives, to our knowledge, the first improvement of the minimum out-degree threshold in two decades, since the work of Kaneko and Locke in 2001, who resolved the conjecture for oriented graphs whose minimum out-degree is at most $6$. The proof is partially computer-assisted: after a sequence of local reductions, the remaining finite obstruction models are eliminated by reproducible OR-Tools CP-SAT infeasibility checks.

**What it claims to prove.**  
Seymour's second neighborhood conjecture (1990) asserts that every oriented graph has a vertex whose second out-neighborhood is at least as large as its first out-neighborhood. The paper verifies this conjecture for the previously-open case of oriented graphs with minimum out-degree exactly 7, extending the threshold up from Kaneko and Locke's 2001 result covering minimum out-degree at most 6. The proof combines structural reduction arguments with a computer-assisted enumeration: after reducing to a finite list of potential obstruction configurations, each is ruled out via constraint-satisfaction (CP-SAT) infeasibility checks.

**Significance.**  
Seymour's second neighborhood conjecture is a famous and stubbornly difficult problem in digraph theory, easy to state but resistant to general attack; despite considerable effort over more than three decades, only small-out-degree cases and special graph classes had been resolved unconditionally, with the general conjecture still wide open. That the minimum-out-degree threshold had not moved past 6 since 2001 underscores how hard even this incremental progress is, and the present paper's advance to out-degree 7 — the first such gain in twenty years — is a genuine, if narrow, achievement. The reliance on computer-assisted case elimination (in the tradition of flag algebras and SAT/CSP-based combinatorial proofs) reflects current best practice for problems of this flavor, but the result establishes only one additional finite case rather than offering a route to the conjecture for general (unbounded) minimum out-degree.

**Rating: C**

### 5. Nearly-uniform degree distributions in spanning subgraphs

**Authors:** Richard Montgomery, Alexey Pokrovskiy, Benny Sudakov  
**arXiv:** [2606.30612](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.30612)

**Abstract.**  
> We show that, when $d=o(n)$, every $d$-regular $n$-vertex graph contains a spanning subgraph whose degree distribution is nearly uniform, i.e., for each $0\leq i\leq d$, there are $(1+o(1))n/(d+1)$ vertices with degree $i$. This proves a conjecture of Alon and Wei on irregular subgraphs and strengthens a previous result of Fox, Luo and Pham.

**What it claims to prove.**  
For any $d$-regular $n$-vertex graph with $d = o(n)$, the paper shows that one can always find a spanning subgraph in which the degrees are spread almost perfectly evenly across all possible values $0,1,\dots,d$ — each degree class capturing a $(1+o(1))/(d+1)$ fraction of the vertices. This confirms a conjecture of Alon and Wei on the existence of such "irregular" (degree-balanced) spanning subgraphs, and it strengthens an earlier partial result of Fox, Luo, and Pham.

**Significance.**  
The question of how evenly degree sequences can be distributed within spanning subgraphs of regular graphs is a natural and clean extremal/probabilistic combinatorics problem, related to a broader family of questions about irregularity and discrepancy in graph subgraph structures. The conjecture had already attracted attention and partial progress from Fox, Luo, and Pham, and its complete resolution here — achieving the best-possible uniform spread across the full degree range — is a satisfying capstone for this specific question. The authors are leading figures in extremal and probabilistic combinatorics, and the result is a clean, fully resolved statement rather than a quantitative improvement on a still-open conjecture; this places it as a solid, well-executed contribution rather than a landmark one, since the underlying problem, while elegant, is not among the field's most central open questions.

**Rating: B**
