### 1. Bisections of graphs under degree constraints

**Authors:** Jie Ma, Hehui Wu  
**arXiv:** [2504.15096](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.15096)

**What it claims to prove.**  
For every graph $G$, the paper proves two results simultaneously: (1) $G$ has a bisection in which every vertex $v$ has at least $(1/4 - o(1))d_G(v)$ neighbours in its own part; and (2) $G$ also has a bisection in which every vertex $v$ has at least $(1/4 - o(1))d_G(v)$ neighbours in the opposite part. Both bounds are asymptotically optimal, matching what random constructions achieve. As a consequence, for every $k \ge 1$ there is a threshold minimum degree $f(k)$ such that every graph with $\delta(G) \ge f(k)$ admits both types of bisection with exactly $k$ intra- and inter-neighbours respectively — the first proof that such thresholds exist at all. A more refined quantitative version is also proved: for any $\varepsilon > 0$ there exist $c_\varepsilon, c'_\varepsilon > 0$ so that minimum degree $c_\varepsilon k$ forces a bisection where every vertex has $\ge k$ same-part neighbours, and almost every vertex additionally has $\ge k$ opposite-part neighbours (and symmetrically). These results extend and sharpen classical graph-partitioning theorems of Erdős, Thomassen, and Kühn–Osthus, while additionally imposing the balance constraint.

**Significance.**  
Graph bisections under local degree constraints had resisted systematic treatment: previous results either controlled intra- or inter-part neighbour counts for balanced bipartitions separately, only for special graph classes, or without the bisection requirement. Achieving both simultaneously for all graphs, with asymptotically tight constants matching random constructions, represents a definitive resolution of the problem. The paper was accepted in *Advances in Combinatorics* (2026:5), one of the leading venues in combinatorics, and the results are strong enough to serve as black-box tools in extremal and structural graph theory. Jie Ma and Hehui Wu are well-established researchers with a consistent record of sharp results in extremal combinatorics.

### 2. Smaller universal posets

**Authors:** Paul Bastide, Carla Groenland, Rajko Nenadov  
**arXiv:** [2509.17820](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.17820)

**What it claims to prove.**  
The paper establishes that there is an absolute constant $C > 0$ such that for every $n \ge 1$, there exists a poset on at most $2^{2n/3 + C\sqrt{n}}$ elements that contains every $n$-element poset as an induced subposet. This improves the classical construction-based upper bound of $2^n$ (embedding every $n$-element poset into the Boolean lattice $2^{[n]}$) by replacing the exponent $n$ with $2n/3 + O(\sqrt{n})$.

**Significance.**  
The universal-poset problem — determining the minimum size of a poset that contains all $n$-element posets as induced subposets — is a classical question in combinatorics analogous to the universal-graph problem for induced subgraphs. The gap between the trivial upper bound $2^n$ and lower bounds had been open for a long time, and reducing the exponent from $n$ to $2n/3$ is a substantial qualitative improvement. The sub-exponential correction $O(\sqrt{n})$ in the exponent further suggests the true answer may be $2^{(2/3 + o(1))n}$, making this paper a likely milestone in settling the asymptotics. Bastide and Groenland are productive early-career researchers with strong contributions to graph containment and structural combinatorics; Nenadov brings probabilistic techniques that are central to the construction.

### 3. Growth of Approximate Groups in Hyperbolic Groups

**Authors:** Michael Saks, Gal Yehuda  
**arXiv:** [2606.13632](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.13632)

**What it claims to prove.**  
The paper proves a growth dichotomy for infinite approximate groups — and more generally approximate semigroups — in hyperbolic groups. Precisely, if $G$ is a finitely generated hyperbolic group and $A \subseteq G$ is infinite with $A^2 \subseteq AX$ for some finite $X \subseteq G$, then either $\langle A \rangle$ is virtually cyclic, or $A$ has positive exponential growth in the ambient word metric. Complementing this, the paper introduces a product-growth criterion: for every hyperbolic group $G$ with generating set $S$ there is a constant $c_{G,S} > 0$ such that $|UV| \ge c_{G,S} |U||V|/(n+k+1)$ for all $U \subseteq B_n$, $V \subseteq B_k$, and this linear-loss bound is optimal in order whenever $G$ contains an element of infinite order. For the free group with its standard generators one may take $c_{G,S} = 1/4$. A further refinement for sphere sets $U \subseteq S_n$, $V \subseteq S_k$ in the free group yields $|UV| \ge (2/3 + 1/(3 \cdot 4^{\min\{n,k\}})) |U||V|$ with the constant shown to be sharp for all $n, k$.

**Significance.**  
Approximate groups — sets with small doubling constants — are central objects in additive combinatorics and geometric group theory, and their structure in non-abelian groups is much less understood than in abelian ones. Proving a sharp dichotomy (virtually cyclic or exponential growth) in all hyperbolic groups is a clean and powerful result that mirrors the Gromov–Tits alternative for subgroups and extends it to the approximate-group setting. The sharp product-growth bounds with explicit constants are of independent interest for geometric and combinatorial applications. Michael Saks is a prominent researcher with deep contributions spanning combinatorics and theoretical computer science; Gal Yehuda is a rising researcher in combinatorics and group theory.

### 4. Proofs of Two Conjectures of Alon on Subgraph Counts

**Authors:** Peiru Kuang, Shuang Sun, Yan Wang, Jiasheng Zeng  
**arXiv:** [2606.18321](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18321)

**What it claims to prove.**  
Let $N(m, H)$ denote the maximum number of subgraphs of a graph $G$ isomorphic to $H$, over all graphs $G$ with $m$ edges; no isolated vertices are allowed throughout. Alon proved that $N(m, H) = \Theta_H(m^{\gamma(H)})$ where $\gamma(H) = (|V(H)| + D(H))/2$ and $D(H) = \max_{S \subseteq V(H)}(|S| - |N_H(S)|)$, and in the same 1986 paper stated two conjectures. Conjecture 1 asked whether $N(m,H)/m^{\gamma(H)}$ converges to a limit as $m \to \infty$. The paper resolves this affirmatively and identifies the limit as $\lambda(H) = \Lambda(H)/|\operatorname{Aut}(H)|$, where $\Lambda(H)$ is the value of an explicit variational problem over finite cores. Conjecture 2 asked whether, when $H$ is a disjoint union of stars, an extremal graph realising $N(m, H)$ can always be taken to be a disjoint union of stars. The paper resolves this affirmatively as well.

**Significance.**  
Both conjectures appear in Alon's 1986 paper in *Israel Journal of Mathematics* and have remained open for forty years. The existence and the explicit identification of the limit $\lambda(H) = \Lambda(H)/|\operatorname{Aut}(H)|$ via a variational problem over finite cores is the deeper of the two results, providing a complete asymptotic picture of extremal subgraph counts and a new connection between graph homomorphism theory and extremal graph theory. The stars conjecture, while more combinatorial in flavour, closes a natural structural question about the form of extremal graphs. Together, these resolutions settle a longstanding chapter in extremal graph theory.

### 5. Ehrhart Theory of the Join of Two Lattice Polytopes

**Authors:** Feihu Liu, Sihao Tao, Guoce Xin  
**arXiv:** [2606.18794](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18794)

**What it claims to prove.**  
The join of two lattice polytopes $P \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$ and $Q \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is the convex hull of $P \times \{0\} \times \{0\}$ and $\{0\} \times Q \times \{1\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{m+n+1}$. The paper resolves the open problem (listed on the AIM open-problems website) of understanding the Ehrhart theory of this join operation. The main results are: (1) explicit counterexamples showing that the join of two Ehrhart-positive polytopes need not be Ehrhart positive; (2) if $P$ and $Q$ both have the integer decomposition property (IDP) and the spanning property, then their join inherits both; (3) the very ample property is not preserved under the join; (4) unimodular triangulations, regular triangulations, and quadratic triangulations are all preserved under the join operation; (5) as a byproduct, a complete necessary and sufficient condition is given for the join of two Gorenstein lattice polytopes to remain Gorenstein.

**Significance.**  
The join is one of the fundamental polytope operations alongside the Cartesian product and the direct sum, but its Ehrhart theory had been essentially unexplored, in contrast to the Cartesian product whose Ehrhart theory is well understood. The mix of positive and negative results — IDP and triangulation properties are preserved, Ehrhart positivity and very-ampleness are not — gives a sharp and nuanced picture. The characterisation of the Gorenstein case provides a useful structural tool. That the problem appeared on the AIM open-problems list indicates it was recognised as a natural and accessible but genuinely open question; its resolution contributes a complete chapter to the combinatorics of lattice polytopes. Guoce Xin is an experienced researcher with prior contributions to enumerative and algebraic combinatorics.
