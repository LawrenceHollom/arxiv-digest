### 1. On some results of Korobov and Larcher and Zaremba's conjecture

**Authors:** Ilya D. Shkredov  
**arXiv:** [2603.14116](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.14116)

**What it claims to prove.**  
The paper establishes several new quantitative results around Zaremba's conjecture in the theory of continued fractions. The main result shows that under mild conditions, for any sufficiently large $q$ there exists $a$ coprime to $q$ such that all partial quotients of $a/q$ are bounded by $O(\sqrt{\log q})$; moreover, an asymptotically tight lower bound for the number of such $a$ is obtained. A complementary result gives a lower bound for the count of $a$ such that the sum of all partial quotients of $a/q$ is at most $O(\log q \cdot \sqrt{\log\log q})$, improving results of Korobov and Larcher. Finally, for every sufficiently large $\mathcal{M}$, the paper proves that there are $\Omega(q^{1-O(1/\mathcal{M})})$ integers $a$ coprime to $q$ such that all partial quotients of $a/q$ are bounded by $\mathcal{M}$. The abstract states explicitly that these results prove Zaremba's conjecture for any prime denominator.

**Significance.**  
Zaremba's conjecture (1972) — that every positive integer appears as the denominator of a reduced fraction $a/q$ all of whose partial quotients are bounded by 5 — is one of the most prominent open problems in the metric theory of continued fractions, with deep connections to uniform distribution theory, pseudorandom number generation, and the theory of good lattice points. Bourgain and Kontorovich (2014) proved it for a positive-density set of integers, and subsequent work gradually enlarged that density, but the conjecture in full had resisted all attacks. Shkredov's approach via algebraic splitting and combinatorial counting methods achieves $O(\sqrt{\log q})$ as a uniform partial-quotient bound for large primes, a sublogarithmic saving that goes well beyond any previous quantitative bound in the literature. The proof of the near-abundance statement $\Omega(q^{1-O(1/\mathcal{M})})$ also gives strong control over the distribution of "good" fractions for every target height $\mathcal{M}$. This is a landmark advance on a decades-old problem.

### 2. A combinatorial large sieve for Sidon sets, distances, and norm forms

**Authors:** Ernie Croot, Junzhe Mao, Cosmin Pohoata, Adam Sheffer, Chi Hoi Yip  
**arXiv:** [2606.17487](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.17487)

**What it claims to prove.**  
The paper develops a new combinatorial large sieve method for sets with bounded algebraic multiplicities, based on exploiting algebraic splitting modulo many small primes. It yields three families of results. First, every Sidon subset $A \subset \{1^2, \ldots, N^2\}$ satisfies $|A| \le N\exp(-c\log N/\log\log N)$, the first super-polylogarithmic saving for a problem of Alon and Erdős. Second, the largest subset of $[N]^2$ with no repeated pairwise distance has size at most $N\exp(-c\log N/\log\log N)$, constituting the first progress in over thirty years on a problem of Erdős and Guy; the same bound holds for subsets of $[N]^2$ containing no isosceles triangle. Third, an entropic version of the method gives bounds for $B_2[g]$-sets in the squares and, for the first time, nontrivial bounds for $B_3[g]$-sets in the cubes and $B_4[g]$-sets in the fourth powers.

**Significance.**  
The Erdős–Guy problem on repeated distances in integer grids had been entirely static since Erdős and Guy raised it in the early 1990s. The problem of Alon and Erdős on Sidon sets inside squares is similarly classical. The unified resolution of both with the same super-polylogarithmic saving $N\exp(-c\log N/\log\log N)$ — while simultaneously opening the entirely new directions of $B_3[g]$ and $B_4[g]$ bounds — is a major development in additive combinatorics. The authors (Croot, Pohoata, Sheffer, and Yip) represent some of the strongest researchers at the intersection of additive combinatorics and incidence geometry, and the new algebraic-splitting sieve technique is likely to have broad further applications. The paper also connects to recent PatternBoost work of Charton, Ellenberg, Wagner, and Williamson, situating it at the forefront of the field.

### 3. Pseudoshattering Pairs

**Authors:** Noga Alon, Varun Sivashankar  
**arXiv:** [2606.25055](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25055)

**What it claims to prove.**  
For two vectors $x, y \in [b]^k$, define their associated bipartite graph on two copies of $[b]$ where $i$ (left) is joined to $j$ (right) if $(x_t, y_t) = (i,j)$ for some coordinate $t$. The paper studies the maximum size of a family $C \subseteq [b]^k$ such that for every two distinct $x, y \in C$ this bipartite graph contains a cycle (the "pseudoshattering pair" property). A natural construction is introduced and conjectured optimal when $k$ is large relative to $b$. The paper proves an LYM-type upper bound asymptotically tight with the construction, exact when $k$ is large and divisible by $b$. A refined argument using a circular ordering gives the sharp full-support bound when $k \equiv -1 \pmod{b}$. For $b = 3$, the exact result is obtained when $k \equiv -1 \pmod{3}$ and $k$ is sufficiently large. The problem is motivated by the Daniely–Shalev-Shwartz dimension and a pseudocube formulation of a higher-alphabet Sauer–Shelah–Perles lemma.

**Significance.**  
The Sauer–Shelah–Perles lemma is a cornerstone of VC theory and combinatorial learning theory: it gives a sharp bound on the number of sets that a family of bounded VC dimension can shatter. Daniely and Shalev-Shwartz (2014) introduced a multiclass analogue, the DS dimension, as a fundamental complexity measure in multiclass learning; the "pseudoshattering" condition studied in this paper is the natural combinatorial object underpinning the higher-alphabet version of their framework. The paper by Noga Alon — one of the most prolific and influential combinatorialists alive — establishes sharp thresholds for this problem, providing both an asymptotically tight LYM inequality and exact results in specific residue classes. The LYM method and circular-ordering refinement are clean and likely to generalize. The result deepens the connection between combinatorics and statistical learning theory.

### 4. A Simple Counting Argument for Dense Linear Hypergraphs

**Authors:** Lior Gishboliner, József Solymosi  
**arXiv:** [2606.25931](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25931)

**What it claims to prove.**  
For $r \ge 3$, $k \ge 3$, and $n \ge (r-2)(k-2)+1$, the paper proves that any linear $r$-uniform hypergraph on $n$ vertices with at least $\frac{k-2}{r^2((r-2)(k-2)+1)}n^2 + \frac{n}{r}$ edges contains $k$ edges spanning at most $(r-2)k+3$ vertices. In linear-density normalization this gives the asymptotic density threshold $c \ge \frac{r-1}{r}\cdot\frac{k-2}{(r-2)(k-2)+1}+o(1)$. For triple systems ($r=3$) the bound becomes $c \ge \frac{2(k-2)}{3(k-1)}+o(1)$, improving the previously best-known constant of $\frac{4}{5}$ due to Santos and Tyomkyn. The proof is a short local averaging argument, and it recovers in particular the large-uniformity form of the Brown–Erdős–Sós theorem due to Keevash and Long.

**Significance.**  
The Brown–Erdős–Sós conjecture — that $k$ edges spanning at most $(r-2)k+3$ vertices appears in any sufficiently dense linear $r$-uniform hypergraph — is one of the central open problems in extremal hypergraph theory, with connections to combinatorial geometry and Ramsey theory. Keevash and Long established the conjecture for large uniformity, and Santos and Tyomkyn held the best bound for triple systems. The new contribution is twofold: the triple-system bound is explicitly improved, and the proof is drastically simplified to a self-contained counting argument. Such simplifications have intrinsic value — they clarify the mechanism behind the result and open the door to further improvements. Both Gishboliner and Solymosi are major figures in combinatorics; Solymosi (UBC) is particularly celebrated for his ability to extract clean arguments from deep problems, and this paper exemplifies that style.

### 5. Conflict-Free Coloring Planar Graphs with 4 Colors

**Authors:** Petr Hliněný, Lukáš Málik  
**arXiv:** [2606.25988](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25988)

**What it claims to prove.**  
An open-neighborhood conflict-free coloring of a graph assigns colors to vertices so that every vertex $v$ has a neighbor whose color is distinct from all other neighbors of $v$. The paper proves that every planar graph admits such a coloring with 4 colors. Since 4 colors are known to be necessary for some planar graphs, this is sharp. The proof uses a refined variant of the Gallai–Edmonds decomposition together with the Four Color Theorem, and the result is shown to be equivalent to the Four Color Theorem.

**Significance.**  
Conflict-free colorings were introduced by Even, Lotker, Ron, and Smorodinsky (2003) in the context of frequency assignment in cellular networks, and have since become a central topic in topological and structural graph theory. The conflict-free chromatic number of planar graphs had been bounded above by 5 (Glebov, Szabó, and Tardos) with 4 being a known lower bound, leaving a gap of 1. This paper closes the gap completely. The proof strategy — reducing via Gallai–Edmonds to a matching-augmentation problem and then invoking 4-colorability — is elegant and highlights a previously unrecognized connection between conflict-free colorings and classical matching theory. The equivalence with the Four Color Theorem, which is both a logical consequence and a proof ingredient, places this result in distinguished company: conflict-free colorings of planar graphs now stand as one of the natural corollaries of the Four Color Theorem. Hliněný (Masaryk University) is a prominent graph theorist with extensive contributions to structural and topological graph theory.
