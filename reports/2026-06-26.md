### 1. Embedding Equitable Rectangles

**Authors:** Amin Bahmanian, Anna Johnsen-Yu  
**arXiv:** [2606.26335](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26335)

**What it claims to prove.**  
Let $M$ be an $n \times n$ array whose top-left $r \times s$ subarray is already filled with symbols from $\{1, 2, \ldots, k\}$ (where $1 \le k \le n^2$). The paper determines necessary and sufficient conditions under which the remaining cells of $M$ can be completed so that each symbol $\ell$ appears exactly $\rho_\ell$ times in total, while the number of occurrences of each symbol in any two rows, and in any two columns, differ by at most one. Equivalently, the result characterizes when a partial edge-coloring of $K_{r,s}$ can be extended to an edge-coloring of $K_{n,n}$ in which each color class is spanning and almost regular with prescribed color-class sizes. As special cases the theorem recovers Hall's marriage theorem (when $s = k = \rho_1 = \cdots = \rho_k = n$), Ryser's completion theorem for Latin rectangles (when $k = \rho_1 = \cdots = \rho_k = n$), and results of Goldwasser–Hilton–Hoffman–Özkan, as well as a theorem of Bahmanian. The bipartite case of Baranyai's construction of almost-regular colorings of complete uniform multipartite hypergraphs is also recovered as a special case.

**Significance.**  
Completion theorems for partial Latin squares and their relatives are among the oldest and most celebrated results in combinatorics. Hall's theorem (1935) and Ryser's completion theorem (1951) are foundational, and Baranyai's theorem (1975) on near-regular hypergraph edge-colorings is one of the deepest results in the area. The fact that a single statement simultaneously unifies all of these — together with the results of Goldwasser, Hilton, Hoffman, Özkan, and Bahmanian — is a genuine structural achievement. The characterization is in terms of a natural system of inequalities on the prescribed frequencies $\rho_\ell$ and on the already-filled cells, making it both clean and verifiable. Bahmanian (Illinois State) is a specialist in design theory and hypergraph decomposition whose prior work on spectrum problems for trades and arrays has a long track record of unifying disparate classical results.

### 2. The Suda–Tanaka–Tokushige Conjecture for $\mathbf{p}$-Biased Intersecting Families

**Authors:** Yongjiang Wu, Lihua Feng  
**arXiv:** [2606.26521](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26521)

**What it claims to prove.**  
For a sequence of probabilities $1 > p_1 \ge \cdots \ge p_n > 0$, let $\mu_{\mathbf{p}}$ be the non-uniform product measure on $2^{[n]}$ defined by $\mu_{\mathbf{p}}(\mathcal{A}) = \sum_{A \in \mathcal{A}} \prod_{i \in A} p_i \prod_{j \notin A}(1-p_j)$. Suda, Tanaka, and Tokushige (2017) conjectured that if $p_3 \le 1/2$ then every intersecting family $\mathcal{A} \subseteq 2^{[n]}$ satisfies $\mu_{\mathbf{p}}(\mathcal{A}) \le p_1$, with equality (under mild non-degeneracy conditions) only for a star centered at an element of maximum weight. The paper proves this conjecture in the following stronger $t$-intersecting form: for any $t \ge 1$, if $p_{t+2} \le \frac{1}{t+1}$, then every $t$-intersecting family satisfies $\mu_{\mathbf{p}}(\mathcal{A}) \le \prod_{i=1}^t p_i$, with equality when $p_{t+2} < \frac{1}{t+1}$ only for a $t$-star $\{A \subseteq [n] : T \subseteq A\}$ achieving the product bound. The result is stated to unify and generalize the classical theorems of Fishburn–Frankl–Freed–Lagarias–Odlyzko and Friedgut.

**Significance.**  
The Erdős–Ko–Rado theorem and its generalizations constitute one of the most active streams in extremal set theory. The non-uniform $p$-biased setting, introduced precisely because real-world probability distributions are not symmetric, has attracted sustained attention since the mid-2010s. Suda, Tanaka, and Tokushige's 2017 conjecture is the natural non-uniform analogue of the classical Frankl $t$-intersecting theorem; proving it in a stronger $t$-intersecting form than originally stated — while simultaneously recovering both the Fishburn–Frankl–Freed–Lagarias–Odlyzko theorem (which covers the equal-weight case) and Friedgut's sharp threshold result — represents a substantial consolidation of the field. The sharp characterization of equality also resolves the extremal problem completely.

### 3. Strong Counterexamples to Mubayi's Supersaturation Conjecture in Every Uniformity

**Authors:** Heng Li, Hong Liu, Xizhi Liu, Jing Wang  
**arXiv:** [2606.26735](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26735)

**What it claims to prove.**  
The classical supersaturation problem asks: given an $r$-graph $\mathcal{F}$ and an $n$-vertex $r$-graph $G$ with $\mathrm{ex}(n,\mathcal{F})+q$ edges, what is the minimum number of copies of $\mathcal{F}$ that must appear in $G$? Mubayi conjectured that for every stable non-$r$-partite $r$-graph $\mathcal{F}$ this minimum is at least $q \cdot c(n, \mathcal{F})$, where $c(n, \mathcal{F})$ is the number of $\mathcal{F}$-copies created by adding a single edge to the extremal $\mathcal{F}$-free $n$-vertex $r$-graph. Ma and Yuan recently refuted the graph case ($r=2$) with counterexamples of arbitrary chromatic number. This paper proves that for every $r \ge 2$ and every $K > 1$ there exists a stable non-$r$-partite $r$-graph $\mathcal{F}$ such that for all large $n$ and every integer $q$ with $1 \le q \le \delta n$, there exists an $n$-vertex $r$-graph with $\mathrm{ex}(n,\mathcal{F})+q$ edges and at most $K^{-1} q \cdot c(n,\mathcal{F})$ copies of $\mathcal{F}$. Thus Mubayi's lower bound can already fail at $q=1$, and the failure can be by an arbitrarily large constant factor, in every uniformity $r \ge 2$.

**Significance.**  
Supersaturation questions originating with Rademacher and Erdős have been central to extremal combinatorics for decades. The Kruskal–Katona theorem and the Frankl–Füredi–Simonovits stability method are among the deepest tools the field has developed precisely to handle them. Mubayi's conjecture, which attempted to pin down the leading coefficient of the minimum copy count, was a natural and widely believed strengthening of stability; the fact that it fails by an arbitrarily large constant factor — and in every uniformity — is a sharp refutation that forces a fundamental reassessment of how supersaturation behaves beyond the Kruskal–Katona regime. Hong Liu (University of Warwick) is one of the most prolific and technically powerful researchers in extremal combinatorics at present; a result from his group carries considerable weight. The construction method is also likely to be useful in other supersaturation problems.

### 4. Proof of the Density Threshold Conjecture for Pinwheel Scheduling

**Authors:** Akitoshi Kawamura  
**arXiv:** [2606.27104](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27104)

**What it claims to prove.**  
In the pinwheel scheduling problem, each task $i$ has a period $a_i \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}$; the goal is to find a perpetual schedule assigning one task per day such that task $i$ is performed at least once every $a_i$ days. The density $\sum_i 1/a_i \le 1$ is a necessary condition. Chan and Chin (1993) conjectured that density at most $5/6$ is also sufficient. The paper proves this conjecture. The proof strategy reduces schedulability to a finite (but large) set of instances via a novel generalization to fractional (non-integer) periods, then verifies those instances by computer search. Byproducts include a simple proof that every two-distinct-period instance of density at most $1$ is schedulable, and a bamboo garden trimming algorithm with approximation ratio $4/3$.

**Significance.**  
The pinwheel scheduling problem, originating in work of Holte, Rosier, Tulchinsky, and Varvel in the late 1980s as a model for real-time task scheduling in satellite communications, has been studied continuously for over thirty years. The $5/6$ density conjecture of Chan and Chin (1993) was the principal open problem in the area; partial results had established the conjecture for special period sequences (two distinct periods, small numbers of tasks, etc.) but the general case had resisted all attempts. The reduction to fractional periods — which makes the feasibility region piecewise-linear and thus finitizable — is the key conceptual step, and it is likely to have applications to other scheduling problems. The $4/3$-approximation for bamboo garden trimming is also a new best result for that related problem.

### 5. Congruent Copies of Finite Patterns in Planar Point Sets

**Authors:** Shubhrajit Bhattacharya, Ritesh Goenka  
**arXiv:** [2606.27352](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.27352)

**What it claims to prove.**  
Given a finite nonempty planar point set $S$, the paper asks for the maximum number of congruent copies of $S$ contained in a set of $n$ points in $\mathbb{R}^2$. The main result is: for every fixed $S$ there exists $\delta_S > 0$ (depending only on $S$) and a construction of $n$-point planar sets containing $\Omega_S(n^{1+\delta_S})$ congruent copies of $S$. This answers in a strong form a question of Brass and Pach, and makes progress on related questions of Erdős–Purdy and Ábrego–Fernández-Merchant. The construction uses the number-field machinery from Sawin's quantitative refinement of the recent OpenAI-team breakthrough on the unit distance problem, and yields an explicit value of $\delta_S$ for every fixed $S$.

**Significance.**  
The unit distance problem — how many pairs among $n$ points in the plane can be at distance exactly $1$ — was Erdős's most famous combinatorial geometry question and remained wide open for over seventy years before the recent OpenAI breakthrough established a super-linear lower bound $\Omega(n^{1+c/\log\log n})$. That result immediately invited a cascade of follow-up questions about other combinatorial geometry problems where similar number-field constructions could be deployed. The problem of Brass and Pach — which asked whether more than $O(n)$ congruent copies of any fixed pattern $S$ can appear in $n$ planar points — is exactly such a problem. Obtaining $\Omega(n^{1+\delta_S})$ for every fixed $S$ constitutes the first superlinear lower bound for all fixed patterns simultaneously, answering the Brass–Pach question and situating this paper at the leading edge of the post-unit-distance wave of results in combinatorial geometry.
